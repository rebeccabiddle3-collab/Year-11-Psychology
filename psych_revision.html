Key Concepts

Revision Guide
📈
Correlation

Pearson's r

Measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two continuous variables.

⚠ Correlation ≠ Causation. A relationship between variables does not mean one causes the other.

Inferential Statistics

P-Values & Significance

The probability the result occurred by chance. A small p = a real effect.

≤ 0.05Moderate✓ Yes

📐
Central Tendency

Mean, Median & Mode

  • Mean — Sum ÷ N. Best for interval/ratio, normally distributed. Sensitive to outliers.
  • Median — Middle value. Best when skewed or outliers present.
  • Mode — Most frequent. Best for nominal data.
x̄ = (x₁ + x₂ + … + xN) ÷ N
Round to 1 decimal place unless stated
📊
Variability

Spread of Data

  • Range — Max − Min.
  • Standard Deviation (SD) — Average spread from mean. Low SD = consistent; High SD = variable.
  • Standard Error (SE) — How well the sample mean represents the population. SE = SD ÷ √n
  • Confidence Interval (CI) — CI = SE × 1.96 (for 95%)
📉
Error Bars

Interpreting Error Bars

Lines on graphs showing variability (SE or CI).

Bars overlap
May NOT be significant
No overlap
May be significant
🔬
Hypothesis Testing

Null vs Alternative

  • H₀ (Null) — Predicts NO significant relationship or difference.
  • H₁ (Alternative) — Predicts a statistically significant relationship or difference.
Reject H₀ when p ≤ 0.05 → support H₁
Retain H₀ when p > 0.05 → no significant effect found

⚠ Never write "proved" or "disproved" — say "supported" or "not supported".

🔁
Reliability

Comparing Reliability

StatisticLow value meansHigh value means
SDScores clustered → reliableScores spread → less reliable
SESample ≈ population meanLess confident in sample

The condition with the lower SD and SE demonstrated the greatest reliability.

SD is a measure of statistical uncertainty — very large SD can suggest the experiment is unreliable or affected by external noise.

💡 Important: Mean and SD are only appropriate for symmetrical data. If data is skewed or has extreme outliers, the mean is pulled away from the centre and SD becomes misleading. Use median and IQR instead.
⚠️
Errors

Type I & Type II Errors

Type I Error

False Positive
Reject H₀ when it IS true. Claiming an effect that doesn't exist.

Type II Error

False Negative
Retain H₀ when it IS false. Missing a real effect.

Normal Distribution

SD & the Bell Curve

  • ≈ 68% of data within 1 SD of mean
  • ≈ 95% of data within 2 SD of mean
  • ≈ 99.7% of data within 3 SD of mean

Normally distributed data: mean = median = mode. Required for parametric tests.

Coefficient of Determination

R² — Explained Variance

R² = r². Shows what proportion of variance in Y is explained by X.

Example: r = −0.78
R² = 0.78² = 0.61
→ 61% of variance in Y explained by X
→ 39% due to other factors
Distribution Shape

Skew

  • Normal — Bell-shaped, mean = median = mode
  • Positive skew (right tail) — Mode < Median < Mean
  • Negative skew (left tail) — Mean < Median < Mode
  • Bimodal — Two peaks in the data
Research Designs

Types of Design

  • Correlational — Tests relationship between two variables. No IV manipulation.
  • Independent groups — Different participants in each condition.
  • Repeated measures — Same participants in all conditions. Needs counterbalancing.
  • Matched participants — Participants matched on key characteristics.
📦
Dispersion

Range, IQR & Quartiles

  • Range = Highest − Lowest score. Simple but affected by outliers.
  • Q1 = 25th percentile (lower quartile)
  • Q2 = 50th percentile (median)
  • Q3 = 75th percentile (upper quartile)
  • IQR = Q3 − Q1. Used for ordinal data instead of SD.
IQR = Q3 − Q1
Variance = average of squared differences from mean
SD = √variance
🌐
Skew

Distribution Shape & Order

  • Normal — Bell-shaped. Mean = Median = Mode.
  • Positive skew (right tail) — Mode < Median < Mean
  • Negative skew (left tail) — Mean < Median < Mode
  • Bimodal — Two peaks in the distribution

When data is skewed → use median, not mean.

💡 MMM Rule: If Mean = Median = Mode → data is normally distributed. If they differ → data is skewed → less certain/reliable.
🎯
Data Quality

Subjective vs Objective Data

Subjective

Based on opinion, feelings, or self-report. No external yardstick. E.g. rating your own stress level.

Objective

Measured against an identifiable external criterion. E.g. standardised tests, reaction time (ms).

🔭
Conclusions

Conclusions & Generalisations

  • A conclusion states whether the hypothesis is supported or rejected based on the data.
  • A generalisation applies findings to the broader population.

To generalise, 3 conditions must be met:

1. Sample must represent the population
2. Results must be statistically significant
3. All confounding variables must be controlled

p-value Interpretation

The threshold is p = 0.05. A p-value tells you the probability the result occurred by chance. p = 0.03 means there is only a 3% chance the result is due to chance alone.

p-valueMeaningDecisionHypothesis
p ≤ 0.05 Result is unlikely due to chance ✓ Statistically significant Reject H₀ → Accept H₁
p > 0.05 Result could be due to chance ✗ Not statistically significant Retain H₀ → Reject H₁
Example — Significant

p = 0.03 → 3% chance result is due to chance → statistically significant → reject H₀

Example — Not Significant

p = 0.12 → 12% chance result is due to chance → not statistically significant → retain H₀

Choosing Statistical Tests

Decision Guide

Step-by-step: Which test do I use?

1
What is your research design?
Correlational

Testing relationship between two variables. No IV is manipulated.

Is data continuous (interval/ratio) & normally distributed?

YES → PARAMETRIC
Pearson's r
Experimental

Comparing groups or conditions. An IV is manipulated.

Same or different participants in each condition?

INDEPENDENT GROUPS
Unpaired t-test
REPEATED MEASURES
Paired t-test
2
Is the data parametric?
Use Parametric when ALL apply:
  • Data is interval or ratio scale
  • Data is symmetrical — approximately normally distributed (no skew)
  • No extreme outliers present
  • Mean and SD are appropriate measures (only valid for symmetrical data)
  • Sample size is reasonably large (ideally n > 30)
💡 Mean and SD are only meaningful for symmetrical data. If data is skewed or has extreme outliers, the mean is pulled away from the centre and becomes misleading.
Pearson's r  |  t-tests
3
What makes Pearson's correlation appropriate for a dataset?

In your exam, justify using Pearson's r by addressing ALL of these:

  • Both variables are continuous (interval or ratio) data
  • The data is symmetrical — approximately normally distributed with no skew
  • No extreme outliers — outliers distort the mean and make Pearson's unreliable
  • The relationship appears linear on a scatterplot
  • Mean and SD are appropriate measures for the data (only valid for symmetrical distributions)
⚠ If the data is skewed or has extreme outliers, Pearson's r is not appropriate because the mean is no longer a reliable measure of centre.
About Each Test
Parametric
t-tests (Paired & Unpaired)
  • Paired t-test — same participants tested twice (repeated measures)
  • Unpaired t-test — different participants per group (independent groups)
  • Produces a t-score with an associated p-value
  • Larger t-score = more difference between groups

The p-value rule
"If the p is low, the null must go —
if the p is high, the null will fly."
🗑️
p is LOW (≤ 0.05)
The null hypothesis must GO — it gets thrown out. Your result is statistically significant. There is less than a 5% chance it happened by chance. Reject H₀, accept H₁.
🦅
p is HIGH (> 0.05)
The null hypothesis gets to fly, flourish, and soar — it survives! Your result is not statistically significant. There's too high a chance it happened by chance alone. Retain H₀.

Cognitive Verbs

Exam Technique
Determine
Work out and give a final answer. Usually a number or one-word answer. No extended explanation needed.
Calculate
Work out mathematically AND show every step of working. Do not just write the final answer.
Identify
Analyse the evidence and briefly state the correct answer. Point to the relevant feature in the data.
Contrast
Compare two things by describing similarities and/or differences. Requires a longer answer referencing both items.
Distinguish
Note key differences between data values. Always include actual numbers (e.g., "SD of 1.56 vs 0.87").
Draw a conclusion
State what the results mean in terms of the original hypothesis. State whether it was supported or rejected. Never write "proven" or "disproven" — psychologists cannot prove a hypothesis, only support or reject it.
Infer
Draw a conclusion based on analysis. Go beyond stating facts — explain what the evidence suggests.
Deduce
Reach a conclusion AND give a reason. Answer = what + why. Must reference data to justify reasoning.
Justify
Give evidence-based reasons for your answer. Always refer to specific values from tables or figures.
Predict
Give an expected result based on available evidence and patterns in the data.

📝 Exam Answer Tips

  • Always reference specific values — e.g., "r = −0.78", "p < 0.0001", "SD = 0.14"
  • Use the structure: Claim → Evidence → Link to hypothesis
  • For significance: state whether p ≤ 0.05 or p > 0.05 and what that means for the hypothesis
  • For error bars: state whether they overlap or not, then state the implication
  • Never write "proved" — always write "supported" or "not supported"
  • For calculate questions: write formula → substitute values → show answer
  • For deduce reliability: compare SD or SE values directly and state which is lower
Answer Templates
Template — Deduce
  1. State your answer clearly.
  2. Reference the specific statistic (e.g. "p = 0.03").
  3. Explain what this means (e.g. "which is less than 0.05").
  4. Link to conclusion (e.g. "therefore the result is statistically significant and H₁ is supported").
Template — Calculate
  1. Write the formula: e.g. x̄ = Σx ÷ N
  2. Substitute the values (show the sum).
  3. Show the division/arithmetic step.
  4. State the final answer with correct rounding and units.
Template — Contrast
  1. State the value for Group A (with actual number).
  2. State the value for Group B (with actual number).
  3. Explain the difference between them.
  4. Interpret what this means (e.g. "indicating Group A has greater variability").
Template — Draw a Conclusion
  1. State the finding (what the data shows).
  2. Reference the evidence (p-value, r-value, error bars).
  3. State which hypothesis is supported or rejected.
  4. Do NOT say "proven" — say "supported" or "rejected".

Glossary

Key Terms
Pearson's r
Statistic measuring strength and direction of a linear relationship between two continuous variables. Ranges −1 to +1.
p-value
Probability an observed difference is due to chance. p ≤ 0.05 = statistically significant.
Null hypothesis (H₀)
Predicts NO statistically significant relationship or difference between variables.
Alternative hypothesis (H₁)
Predicts a statistically significant relationship or difference between variables.
Mean (x̄)
Arithmetic average. Sum all values then divide by N. Sensitive to outliers.
Median
Middle value when data is ordered. Not affected by extreme values — best for skewed data.
Mode
Most frequently occurring value. A dataset can be bimodal or multimodal. Best for nominal data.
Standard Deviation (SD)
Average distance of scores from the mean. Low SD = consistent; High SD = spread out.
Standard Error (SE)
How well the sample mean represents the population mean. SE = SD ÷ √n. Smaller SE = more reliable.
Confidence Interval (CI)
Range where population mean likely falls. 95% CI = SE × 1.96. Used as error bars on graphs.
Statistical significance
Result is significant when p ≤ 0.05, meaning unlikely to have occurred by chance alone.
Error bars
Lines on graphs showing variability (SE or CI). Overlapping bars suggest the difference may NOT be significant. Non-overlapping bars suggest the difference MAY be significant — but always confirm with a p-value.
Correlational design
Research design investigating the relationship between two variables without manipulating an IV. Cannot show causation.
Independent groups design
Different participants assigned to each condition. Each person participates in only one group.
Repeated measures design
Same participants take part in every condition. Needs counterbalancing to control order effects.
Type I Error (False Positive)
Rejecting H₀ when it is actually true. Claiming a significant effect that doesn't really exist.
Type II Error (False Negative)
Retaining H₀ when it is actually false. Failing to detect a real effect.
R² (Coefficient of Determination)
R² = r². Shows what proportion of variance in Y is explained by X. E.g., R²=0.61 → 61% explained.
Normal distribution
Bell-shaped, symmetrical distribution where mean = median = mode. Required for parametric tests.
Parametric test
Assumes normal distribution and interval/ratio data. E.g., Pearson's r, t-tests.
Hippocampus
Brain region involved in spatial memory and navigation. Can increase in volume with sustained use — a key example of neuroplasticity.
Selective attention
Focusing on one specific stimulus or task while ignoring others. Associated with faster reaction times.
Divided attention
Attempting to attend to more than one stimulus or task simultaneously. Generally leads to reduced performance.

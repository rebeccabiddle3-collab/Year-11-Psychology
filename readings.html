🧠 Unit 1 · Topic 1 — The Role of the Brain

🔬
Maguire, E.A. et al. (2006) — London Taxi Drivers and Bus Drivers
Structural MRI study comparing the hippocampi of London taxi drivers (who must memorise the A–Z street map) with bus drivers. Found significantly larger posterior hippocampal grey matter in taxi drivers, with volume correlated with years of experience. Key evidence for experience-dependent neural plasticity and neuroimaging applications. Used in QCAA sample assessment instruments for the neuroimaging dot point.
Maguire, E.A., Woollett, K., & Spiers, H.J. (2006). London taxi drivers and bus drivers: A structural MRI and neuropsychological analysis. Hippocampus, 16(12), 1091–1101. https://doi.org/10.1002/hipo.20233
U1 T1 ✅ QCAA sample instrument — strongly recommended
📄 Open PDF
🔬
Penfield & Rasmussen (1950) — The Cerebral Cortex of Man
Original research establishing the sensory and motor homunculus through electrical stimulation of the brain (ESB) during the Montreal Procedure. Core source for the syllabus dot point: "describe early brain investigative techniques including… experimental neurosurgery (refer to… Wilder Penfield)".
Penfield, W., & Rasmussen, T. (1950). The cerebral cortex of man: A clinical study of localisation of function. Macmillan.

🧩 Unit 1 · Topic 2 — Cognitive Development

🔬
Lorenz, K. (1937) — The Companion in the Bird's World
Lorenz's foundational study on imprinting in greylag geese — demonstrating that attachment bonds form to the first moving object seen after hatching, regardless of species. Introduced the concept of the critical period for attachment. The syllabus dot point explicitly names Konrad Lorenz (1937).
Lorenz, K. (1937). The companion in the bird's world. The Auk, 54(3), 245–273.
U1 T2 ✅ Mandatory — named in syllabus
📄 Open PDF
🔬
Harlow & Zimmermann (1958) — The Development of Affective Responsiveness in Infant Monkeys
Classic rhesus monkey study demonstrating that infant attachment is driven by contact comfort, not food. Infant monkeys consistently preferred the soft cloth surrogate over the wire (food-providing) surrogate — directly challenging behaviourist drive-reduction theory of attachment. Explicitly named in syllabus: "Harry Harlow (Harlow & Zimmermann 1958)".
Harlow, H. F., & Zimmermann, R. R. (1958). The development of affective responsiveness in infant monkeys. Proceedings of the American Philosophical Society, 102(5), 501–509.
U1 T2 ✅ Mandatory — named in syllabus
📄 Open PDF
🔬
Bowlby, J. (1969) — Attachment and Loss, Vol. 1
Bowlby's landmark evolutionary-ethological theory of attachment — introducing the internal working model, monotropy, and the concept that disrupted early attachment (maternal deprivation) has lasting developmental consequences. Explicitly named in syllabus: "John Bowlby (1969)".
Bowlby, J. (1969). Attachment and loss: Vol. 1. Attachment. Basic Books.
U1 T2 ✅ Mandatory — named in syllabus
📄 Open PDF
🔬
Ainsworth, Blehar, Waters & Wall (1978) — Patterns of Attachment
Ainsworth's Strange Situation procedure and classification of three primary infant attachment styles: secure, anxious-avoidant, and anxious-ambivalent (a fourth, disorganised, was added by Main & Solomon 1986). The definitive empirical operationalisation of Bowlby's theory. Explicitly named in syllabus: "Mary Ainsworth (Ainsworth, Blehar, Waters & Wall 1978)".
Ainsworth, M. D. S., Blehar, M. C., Waters, E., & Wall, S. (1978). Patterns of attachment: A psychological study of the strange situation. Erlbaum.
U1 T2 ✅ Mandatory — named in syllabus
📄 Open PDF
🔬
Piaget, J. (1936) — Origins of Intelligence in the Child
Piaget's foundational account of cognitive development through four stages (sensorimotor, preoperational, concrete operational, formal operational), built on core concepts of schemas, assimilation, accommodation and equilibration. Explicitly named in syllabus: "cognitive… Jean Piaget (1936)".
Piaget, J. (1936). Origins of intelligence in children. International Universities Press.
U1 T2 ✅ Mandatory — named in syllabus
📄 Open PDF
🔬
Vygotsky, L. (1978) — Mind in Society
Vygotsky's sociocultural theory of cognitive development — introducing the Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD), the More Knowledgeable Other (MKO), and scaffolding. Argues that all higher cognitive functions originate in social interaction. Explicitly named in syllabus: "sociocultural… Lev Vygotsky (1978)".
Vygotsky, L. S. (1978). Mind in society: The development of higher psychological processes. Harvard University Press.

📄 Open PDF
🔬
Rutter, M. (2004) — Deprivation of Maternal Care: A Reassessment of its Effects
Rutter's critical reassessment of the evidence on early abuse and deprivation — distinguishing between different types of deprivation and their specific cognitive and developmental outcomes. The 2025 syllabus explicitly names Michael Rutter (2004) as the required reference for the dot point: "understand that early abuse or deprivation can have detrimental effects on cognitive development (Michael Rutter 2004)". This is a mandatory named reading that is currently missing from your repository.
Rutter, M. (2004). Deprivation of maternal care: A reassessment of its effects. Schocken Books / WHO.

🌙 Unit 1 · Topic 3 — Consciousness, Attention & Sleep

🔬
Craik, Govoni, Naveh-Benjamin & Anderson (1996) — The Effects of Divided Attention on Encoding and Retrieval Processes
Foundational study demonstrating that divided attention at encoding severely impairs later free recall, while divided attention at retrieval has a smaller, more selective effect. This is the basis of the mandatory Topic 3 practical investigation — students replicate and modify core aspects of this procedure to generate their own data for FIA2.
Craik, F. I. M., Govoni, R., Naveh-Benjamin, M., & Anderson, N. D. (1996). The effects of divided attention on encoding and retrieval processes in human memory. Journal of Experimental Psychology: General, 125(2), 159–180.
U1 T3 ✅ Mandatory — named in syllabus 🧪 Practical basis 📋 FIA2 foundation
